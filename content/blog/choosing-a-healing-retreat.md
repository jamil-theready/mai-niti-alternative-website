The decision to attend a healing retreat in Peru is significant. Where you choose to do this work can mean the difference between a safe, transformative experience and one that is harmful or empty. The landscape of retreat centers has grown rapidly in recent years, and not all operate with the same standards of safety, ethics, and authenticity.

## Safety First

This is non negotiable. Before anything else, evaluate the safety protocols of any center you consider. Ask these questions:

- Do they screen participants for medical and psychiatric conditions?
- Do they require a complete medication disclosure?
- Do they have a clear policy on pharmaceutical interactions?
- Is there medical support available on site or nearby?
- What is their emergency protocol?
- What is the staff to guest ratio during ceremonies?

A center that does not ask about your medications is a center that is not prioritizing your safety. Walk away from any program that skips medical screening.

## Lineage and Authenticity

Plant medicine is not a generic product. It exists within specific cultural and spiritual traditions. The [Shipibo tradition](/blog/the-shipibo-healing-tradition), for example, has its own protocols, songs, and methods that have been refined over generations.

Ask about the curanderos. Where did they train? How long have they been practicing? Do they come from a lineage, or are they self taught? A curandero with 20 years of traditional training under a master is different from someone who attended a few workshops and decided to lead ceremonies.

This is not about gatekeeping. It is about your safety and the depth of your experience. [The role of the curandero](/blog/the-role-of-the-curandero) is critical. Their training determines their ability to hold space, navigate difficult experiences, and provide genuine healing.

## Ethical Practices

How does the center treat its indigenous staff? Are the curanderos fairly compensated? Does the center contribute to the local community? These questions matter.

The plant medicine space has a troubling history of exploitation. Indigenous healers have been underpaid, overworked, and had their knowledge extracted without proper compensation or credit. [Respecting indigenous traditions](/blog/respecting-indigenous-traditions) means choosing centers that model ethical relationships.

Ask about the center's relationship with the surrounding community. Do they employ local people? Do they support local schools or conservation efforts? A center that profits from indigenous knowledge while giving nothing back is not one you want to support.

## The Importance of the Dieta

Does the center take the [dieta](/blog/understanding-the-dieta) seriously? A retreat that lets you eat whatever you want and drink alcohol between ceremonies is not operating within traditional protocol. The dieta is not optional. It is foundational to safe and effective work with plant medicine.

Look for centers that provide clear dieta guidelines in advance, prepare appropriate meals during the retreat, and advise on post retreat dietary care.

## Integration Support

What happens after the retreat? The best centers offer [integration support](/blog/integration-after-ceremony) of some kind. This might be follow up calls, online group sessions, referrals to integration therapists, or written resources. A center that sends you home with no support after intense ceremonial work is not completing its duty of care.

## Size and Atmosphere

Some centers accommodate 30 or more guests at a time. Others work with small groups of 8 to 12. There is no universally right number, but consider what environment will support your healing.

Larger groups can feel less personal. The curandero's attention is divided among more people. Smaller groups allow for more individualized care and a deeper sense of [community](/blog/community-and-healing) among participants.

## Red Flags

Watch for these warning signs:

- Guarantees of specific outcomes ("We will cure your depression")
- No medical screening process
- Unwillingness to answer questions about their curanderos' training
- Very low prices that suggest underpaid staff
- High volume, factory style operations
- Pressure tactics or urgency in their marketing
- No clear emergency or safety protocols

## Trust Your Instincts

After you have done your research, trust your gut. Read reviews. Talk to alumni. Visit the center's website and see how it feels. The right center for your first experience should feel safe, honest, and grounded.

## Taking Your Time With This Decision

There is no rush. The right retreat will still be there when you are ready. Spend weeks or even months researching. Reach out to centers with specific questions. Pay attention to how they respond. A center that takes time to answer thoughtfully, that does not pressure you to book immediately, and that demonstrates genuine care for your wellbeing before you have paid anything is showing you how they will treat you when you arrive. Trust that instinct. The quality of your healing experience begins long before you board the plane. It begins with the quality of your discernment in choosing where to go and who to trust with your process.

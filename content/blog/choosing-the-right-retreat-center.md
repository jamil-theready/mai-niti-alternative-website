## Why Your Choice of Center Matters

The retreat center you choose shapes every aspect of your plant medicine experience. The quality of the healers, the safety protocols, the physical environment, the food, the support staff, and the overall integrity of the operation all influence whether your retreat produces genuine healing or becomes a cautionary tale.

The plant medicine tourism industry in Peru and beyond has grown rapidly. This growth has attracted both deeply committed practitioners and opportunists looking to profit from Western demand. The gap between the best and worst retreat centers is enormous. At the top end, you find centers run by respected indigenous healers with decades of experience, proper medical screening, trained support staff, and genuine dedication to participant welfare. At the bottom end, you find untrained facilitators, no safety protocols, and potentially dangerous conditions.

## The Stakes Are Real

Plant medicine ceremony is not a spa day. It is a powerful intervention that temporarily alters consciousness, produces intense physical and emotional responses, and opens people to states of extreme vulnerability. In the hands of a skilled, ethical healer in a well-run center, this vulnerability becomes the doorway to healing. In the hands of an unqualified or unethical operator, it becomes a risk.

People have been physically harmed, psychologically destabilized, and sexually assaulted at poorly run retreat centers. These incidents, while not representative of the broader industry, are preventable. Thorough research, clear-eyed evaluation, and a willingness to walk away from centers that do not meet basic standards of [safety](/blog/safety-at-plant-medicine-retreats) are your best protection.

## Green Flags: What to Look For

Transparency is the single most important green flag. A trustworthy center is open about who their healers are, where they trained, and how long they have been practicing. They will share information about their safety protocols, medical screening process, and emergency procedures without being asked. They have nothing to hide and everything to demonstrate.

Look for centers that conduct thorough medical intake. A detailed health questionnaire, questions about medications, psychiatric history, and heart conditions, should be standard. If a center lets you book without asking about your medical history, they are not taking your safety seriously. The screening process protects both you and the other participants.

## Healer Credentials and Lineage

The healers are the heart of any retreat. Look for centers that identify their [curanderos](/blog/role-of-the-curandero) by name and describe their training background. Legitimate Shipibo healers have typically trained for a minimum of 10 to 15 years, often much longer, through an [apprenticeship](/blog/apprenticeship-in-shipibo-medicine) system that involves extensive plant dietas, supervised ceremony practice, and gradual assumption of responsibility under their teacher's guidance.

A center that vaguely mentions "experienced shamans" without naming them or describing their lineage is hiding something. Similarly, beware of centers where the "healer" is a Westerner who attended a few retreats and decided to start leading ceremonies. There is a vast difference between having participated in ceremony and being qualified to lead one. The former takes weeks. The latter takes decades.

## Red Flags: What Should Concern You

The most dangerous red flag is a center that does not conduct medical screening. Without screening, people with dangerous contraindications, such as certain heart conditions or incompatible medications, can be put at serious risk. No legitimate center skips this step. Ever.

Watch for centers that promise guaranteed outcomes. Statements like "we cure depression" or "100% success rate" are dishonest. No healer and no medicine can guarantee specific results. Plant medicine works differently for each person, and the outcome depends on many factors including the participant's readiness, the healer's skill, and forces that neither party fully controls. A center that promises guaranteed healing is prioritizing marketing over truth.

## Boundary Violations and Isolation

Any center where male healers work with female participants alone, without support staff present, should be approached with extreme caution. Reports of sexual abuse in plant medicine settings almost always involve situations where boundaries around healer-participant contact were absent or deliberately ignored. Ethical centers have clear policies about physical contact, gender dynamics in ceremony, and the presence of support staff at all times.

Be wary of centers that isolate participants from communication with the outside world without clear therapeutic justification. While limiting phone use during retreat has legitimate benefits, a center that confiscates passports, prevents participants from leaving, or discourages contact with family is displaying cult-like behavior. You should always be free to leave a retreat if you choose to. That freedom is non-negotiable, regardless of what you signed or paid.

## Questions to Ask Before Booking

Before committing financially, send the center a list of direct questions and evaluate how they respond. The quality of their response tells you as much as the answers themselves. A center that responds promptly, thoroughly, and without defensiveness demonstrates professionalism and confidence in their operation.

Ask about healer qualifications. Who will lead ceremony? How long have they practiced? Who trained them? Ask about the facilitator-to-participant ratio. One healer for 20 participants is insufficient. Look for ratios of one healer or facilitator for every four to six participants. Ask what happens if someone has a medical emergency. Is there a medical professional on site or on call? How far is the nearest hospital?

## Logistics and Aftercare

Ask about the daily schedule. How many ceremonies are included? What other healing modalities are offered? What does a typical day between ceremonies look like? Ask about food and dietary accommodations. Ask about the physical accommodations. Private room or shared? Bathroom situation? Electricity and hot water availability?

Ask about post-retreat support. Does the center offer [integration](/blog/returning-home-after-retreat) resources? Follow-up calls? Referrals to integration therapists? A center that invests in aftercare demonstrates understanding that the retreat is the beginning of the healing process, not the entire process. Centers that collect payment and disappear after your departure date are missing a crucial piece of the healing picture. The best centers maintain a relationship with former participants because they genuinely care about long-term outcomes, not just short-term revenue.

## Trusting Your Gut

After all the research, questionnaires, and email exchanges, trust your instinct. If something feels off about a center, even if you cannot articulate exactly what, honor that feeling. Your intuition is a valid data point, especially when you are about to enter a state of deep vulnerability.

Read reviews across multiple platforms. A handful of glowing testimonials on a center's own website mean very little. Look for reviews on independent platforms, in [plant medicine community](/blog/plant-medicine-for-spiritual-seekers) forums, and on social media. Pay attention to how the center responds to negative reviews. A defensive or dismissive response to criticism tells you something important about their character.

## The Investment Perspective

A plant medicine retreat is a significant investment of time, money, and emotional energy. The financial cost is the least important of these three. Getting the decision wrong costs more than money. It can cost your sense of safety, your psychological stability, and your trust in a healing modality that might otherwise serve you profoundly.

Do not choose based on price alone. The cheapest option is often cheap for a reason: underpaid or undertrained healers, poor facilities, inadequate safety measures. But the most expensive option is not automatically the best either. Some premium-priced centers invest more in marketing than in healer compensation and safety infrastructure. Look past the price tag and the Instagram photos. Evaluate the substance beneath the surface. A [center in Peru](/blog/plant-medicine-retreats-in-peru) with humble facilities, a respected healer, and genuine care for participants is worth more than a luxury resort with a photo-ready ceremony space and a facilitator who completed a weekend training course.
